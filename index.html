About Polar Bears

Polar bears are large carnivorous mammals native to the Arctic Circle. They are superbly adapted to life in the cold and icy environment of the Arctic.

Known for their white fur, which provides camouflage in their snowy habitat, polar bears are also excellent swimmers and can travel long distances over water.

They primarily feed on seals, relying on sea ice for hunting. Polar bears are considered marine mammals because of their dependence on the ocean.

Conservation Efforts

Due to climate change and habitat loss, polar bears are classified as vulnerable by the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species. Several conservation efforts are in place to protect them:

  • Monitoring polar bear populations and habitats
  • Reducing greenhouse gas emissions to mitigate climate change
  • Educating communities and raising awareness about polar bear conservation
  • Supporting research on polar bear behavior, ecology, and health

Fun Facts

  • Polar bears are the largest land carnivores in the world.
  • They have an excellent sense of smell, which helps them detect seals under the ice from far distances.
  • Adult male polar bears can weigh up to 1,500 pounds (680 kilograms).
  • Polar bears' fur appears white, but it's actually transparent with a hollow core that reflects light.
  • They spend most of their lives on sea ice, hunting seals and other marine mammals.
